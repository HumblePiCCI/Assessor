In "The Borrowed Name," Jordan uses a fake screen name online to escape judgment and to feel more in control. The author develops a clear message: when identity becomes a performance, the cost is not just a possible exposure, but the internal pressure of maintaining two versions of the self.

Jordan begins by minimizing the choice. "It was just a screen name, not a lie," Jordan claims, drawing a thin line between anonymity and dishonesty. The author uses this rationalization to reveal how people make risky choices feel acceptable. Jordan feels temporary confidence, but it is built on a condition: the truth must stay hidden.

As the story unfolds, the author shifts the consequence from external danger to internal strain. Jordan admits, "My real name felt heavy in my mouth." This metaphor suggests that honesty, which should feel natural, has become difficult because Jordan has practiced avoidance. Jordan's identity is no longer stable; it depends on context and concealment.

When Jordan recognizes a classmate's voice in the forum, the two worlds collide. The online space stops being a separate stage. Jordan becomes silent, avoiding both school conversations and the forum. This reaction shows the real consequence of the borrowed name: instead of creating freedom, it produces isolation and mistrust.

In the conclusion, Jordan chooses to reveal the truth, not as a dramatic confession, but as a refusal to keep performing. The author implies that integrity is not about never making mistakes; it is about ending the cycle of self-justification and returning to a consistent self.

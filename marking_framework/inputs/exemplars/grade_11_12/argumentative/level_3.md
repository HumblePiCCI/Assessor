Schools should permit AI writing tools with clear disclosure and structured boundaries. A ban is understandable, but it treats the technology as a moral emergency rather than a tool that requires literacy. If schools want honest learning, they should teach students how to use AI responsibly and how to preserve authorship.

First, AI can support learning when it is used as assistance rather than substitution. Students can use tools to brainstorm, clarify a sentence, or identify patterns in grammar mistakes. For some learners, this functions like a scaffold: it lowers the friction of writing so that the student can focus on argument and evidence. In that sense, AI can reduce inequality by providing feedback to students who do not have access to private tutoring.

Second, AI literacy is now part of digital literacy. Students will encounter AI tools in post-secondary contexts and workplaces, and they will need to understand limits, bias, and accountability. If schools avoid the topic, students will still use the tools, but they will do so secretly and without guidance. A policy that allows AI with disclosure creates space for instruction and ethical habits.

The main counterargument is academic integrity: students might submit AI-generated work as their own. This risk is real, but it is not solved by prohibition alone. Schools can require process evidence (drafts, planning notes, in-class checkpoints) and can ask students to justify choices verbally. These practices measure learning directly.

Ultimately, the goal is not "AI or no AI." The goal is truthful assessment of student thinking. Allowing AI with disclosure and process-based expectations is a more realistic path to that goal than a simple ban.

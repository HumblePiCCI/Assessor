Schools should allow AI writing tools under a disclosure-based policy with clear constraints. Prohibition is tempting because it appears to protect academic integrity, but in practice it often drives use underground. A better approach teaches AI literacy while preserving authentic assessment of student thinking.

AI can function as a scaffold when it supports, rather than replaces, the writing process. Students can use tools to generate outlines, clarify phrasing, or identify recurring grammar errors. For learners who struggle with structure or language conventions, that support can reduce barriers and allow the student to focus on argument, evidence, and reasoning. This is similar to other forms of feedback, but it must be governed by explicit expectations.

AI literacy is also now an educational responsibility. Students will encounter AI tools in universities and workplaces. If school treats AI as taboo, students learn only secrecy. A policy that allows AI with disclosure creates room to teach limitations (hallucinations, bias, overconfidence) and to reinforce accountability: the student remains responsible for the claims and quality of the final work.

The strongest objection is that AI enables plagiarism-like behavior. That concern is valid, but a ban does not guarantee compliance. Instead, schools can measure learning through process: drafts, annotated sources, in-class writing checkpoints, and brief conferences where students explain key decisions. These methods reduce the incentive and ability to outsource thinking while still permitting limited AI support.

In short, schools should treat AI the way they treat other powerful tools: by teaching responsible use, requiring transparency, and designing assessments that evaluate thinking, not just polished text.

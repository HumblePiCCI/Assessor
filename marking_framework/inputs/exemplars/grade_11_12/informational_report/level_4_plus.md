Peer review is a structured evaluation process used in academic publishing to assess the quality of research before it becomes part of the scientific record. Experts review a manuscript for methodological rigor, logical coherence, and the relationship between evidence and claims. Peer review is not a guarantee of truth, but it is a deliberate friction point designed to slow weak work and strengthen strong work through critique.

How peer review functions
After a researcher submits a manuscript to a journal, an editor performs an initial screening for relevance and basic standards. If the paper passes, the editor invites reviewers with domain expertise. Reviewers examine the research question, study design, measurement choices, data analysis, and interpretation. They assess whether the conclusions are warranted and whether limitations are acknowledged rather than hidden.

Revision as a central mechanism
The most common outcome is "revise and resubmit." Reviewers may request clearer definitions, stronger justification for methods, alternative analyses, or acknowledgment of confounds. Authors respond in a revision letter, addressing comments point-by-point. This exchange can improve the work by forcing precision and by exposing weak reasoning.

Strengths and constraints
Peer review increases reliability by introducing external scrutiny and by filtering out clearly unsupported claims. However, it is constrained by human factors: reviewer disagreement, limited time, and potential bias. Because of these limitations, scientific reliability also depends on replication, data transparency, and ongoing post-publication critique. In other words, peer review is a gate, not the entire fortress.

Conclusion
Peer review is best understood as an institutionalized conversation about evidence. Its purpose is not perfection, but disciplined skepticism, which helps the scientific community approach more dependable knowledge over time.

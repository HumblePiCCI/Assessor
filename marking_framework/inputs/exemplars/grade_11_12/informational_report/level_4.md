Peer review is a formal evaluation process used in academic publishing to assess research quality before publication. Experts in a field review a manuscript for methodological soundness, clarity, and alignment between evidence and claims. While peer review cannot guarantee correctness, it acts as a structured checkpoint that reduces obvious errors and improves the quality of communication.

Process overview
Researchers submit a manuscript to a journal, where an editor completes an initial screening. If the paper fits the journal's scope and meets baseline standards, the editor invites peer reviewers with relevant expertise. Reviewers evaluate the research question, methods, data analysis, and reasoning. They look for gaps such as unsupported claims, unclear variables, or overlooked limitations.

Decisions and revisions
Most papers are not accepted immediately. Reviewers often request revisions, ranging from minor edits (clarifying definitions, improving structure) to major changes (re-analyzing data, addressing confounding factors). Authors respond with a revision letter that explains how each comment was handled. The editor then decides whether the revised paper is acceptable.

Value and limitations
Peer review improves research by adding external scrutiny and by pushing authors to justify methods and conclusions. However, it can still miss errors, and reviewer disagreement or bias can shape outcomes. For that reason, peer review should be viewed as one layer in a broader system that includes transparency, replication, and post-publication critique.

Conclusion
Peer review strengthens the reliability of published research by forcing researchers to defend and clarify their work. Its value is highest when combined with ongoing verification through replication and open scientific discussion.

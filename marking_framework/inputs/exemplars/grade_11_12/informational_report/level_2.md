Peer review is a process used in academic publishing where experts evaluate a study before it is published. The goal is to check quality and reduce errors, although it does not guarantee perfection.

When a researcher finishes a paper, they submit it to a journal. The journal editor decides if it fits the journal and then sends it to reviewers. Reviewers are usually people who study the same topic. They read the paper and provide feedback on the methods, evidence, and conclusions.

Reviewers may recommend accepting the paper, rejecting it, or asking for revisions. Revisions can include clarifying explanations, adding evidence, or fixing problems in the study design. After the author responds, the editor makes a final decision.

Peer review matters because it adds a checkpoint before information becomes widely shared. It can catch mistakes and improve clarity. However, peer review can still miss errors, and reviewers can disagree. For this reason, scientific knowledge also depends on repetition and further research over time.
